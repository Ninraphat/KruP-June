Section C — Grammar Spotlight

The four building blocks of every English sentence.

S
Subject

Subject

The person, animal, thing, or idea that the sentence is about. The subject does the action or is described.

ประธาน — ผู้กระทำหรือสิ่งที่ถูกพูดถึง
Ask: "Who or what does the action?"
Position: Usually at the start
V
Verb

Verb

The action or state in the sentence. Every sentence must have a verb. Verbs can show actions (run, eat) or states (be, seem, feel).

กริยา — การกระทำหรือสภาพ
Ask: "What does the subject do or feel?"
Types: Action verb / Linking verb
O
Object

Object

The receiver of the action. Follows an action verb. Can be Direct (what?) or Indirect (to whom?).

กรรม — ผู้ถูกกระทำหรือสิ่งที่รับการกระทำ
Direct O: "What/Whom does the subject verb?"
Indirect O: "To whom / For whom?"
C
Complement

Complement

A word that describes or renames the subject or object. Always follows a linking verb (be, seem, look, feel, become, sound, taste).

ส่วนเติมเต็ม — คำที่ขยายหรือบอกลักษณะของประธาน/กรรม
Ask: "What is the subject / object like?"
Types: Noun / Adjective after linking verb
⚡
Object vs. Complement — The Key Difference:
An Object is a different person or thing that receives the action: "She loves music." (music ≠ she)
A Complement describes or renames the subject itself: "She is a musician." (musician = she)
🔗
Common Linking Verbs (always followed by Complement, never Object):
be (is, am, are, was, were)  ·  become  ·  seem  ·  look  ·  sound  ·  feel  ·  taste  ·  smell  ·  appear  ·  remain

เป็น อยู่ คือ / กลายเป็น / ดู / ฟังดู / รู้สึก / รสชาติ / กลิ่น / ดูเหมือน / ยังคงเป็น

Error Correction

Find the mistake in each sentence. Click "Show Correction" to see the right answer.

📌 Each sentence has ONE error related to sentence structure. Find it and fix it!
1. She gave to me the homework yesterday. ❌ to me
✅ She gave me the homework yesterday.
When IO comes before DO, do NOT use "to". Use "to" only when IO comes after DO: "She gave the homework to me."
2. The coffee tastes bitterly. ❌ bitterly
✅ The coffee tastes bitter.
After a linking verb (tastes, smells, sounds, feels), use an adjective, not an adverb. tastes bitter ✓ (not bitterly ✗)
3. My parents bought for me a new laptop. ❌ for me
✅ My parents bought me a new laptop. OR My parents bought a new laptop for me.
"For me" can only come AFTER the direct object. When IO comes first, no preposition is used.
4. They elected him as a president. ❌ as a president
✅ They elected him president.
With verbs like elect, appoint, call, name — the Object Complement does NOT need "as". Write it directly after the object.
5. The news made her sadly. ❌ sadly
✅ The news made her sad.
In Pattern S + V + O + C, the object complement must be an adjective, not an adverb. made her sad ✓ (not sadly ✗)

Section F — Unit Review

Key rules to remember + your self-evaluation checklist.

📋 Unit 1 — Key Rules Summary

🔵 Subject

  • Who or what the sentence is about
  • Always before the main verb
  • Can be a noun, pronoun, or noun phrase
  • Ask: "Who/What + verb?"

🟢 Verb

  • Shows action or state
  • Every sentence MUST have a verb
  • Action verbs → take Objects
  • Linking verbs → take Complements

🟡 Object

  • Receives the action (after action verb)
  • Direct Object: Ask "What/Whom?"
  • Indirect Object: Ask "To/For whom?"
  • IO before DO = no preposition needed

🩷 Complement

  • Describes or renames S or O
  • Follows a linking verb only
  • Always use adjective (not adverb!)
  • Subject C = describes subject; Object C = describes object
🚨
Top 5 Most Common Errors (ข้อผิดพลาดที่พบบ่อย):
  1. Using adverb after linking verb: tastes bitterly ✗ → tastes bitter ✓
  2. Adding "as" with object complement: elected him as president ✗ → elected him president ✓
  3. Putting "to" before IO: gave to me a gift ✗ → gave me a gift ✓
  4. Using subject pronoun as object: Tell they ✗ → Tell them ✓
  5. Confusing O with C after linking verb: She became it ✗ → She became a doctor ✓
✅ Self-Evaluation Checklist — "I Can…"
  • Identify Subject, Verb, Object, and Complement in any sentence.
  • Explain the difference between an Object and a Complement.
  • Name at least 5 common linking verbs.
  • Use an adjective (not adverb) correctly after a linking verb.
  • Tell apart Direct Object and Indirect Object.
  • Rewrite a Pattern 4 sentence using "to" or "for" (Pattern 6).
  • Recognise all 6 sentence patterns from examples.
  • Complete a 10-blank cloze using sentence structure knowledge.
  • Answer 6 exam-style MCQ on sentence structure correctly.
