Phase Two

Curriculum Audit & Problem Diagnosis

2.1 The Five Root Causes

Problem 1: No Reading Strand

Your curriculum contains zero reading passages. In the Genius exam, reading comprehension is tested in at least 2 question types (Reading + Cloze), often accounting for 15–20 questions (25–35% of the exam). Students cannot transfer grammar knowledge to unseen texts if they have never practiced doing so.

📌Impact: Students may correctly fill in grammar blanks in isolation but fail when grammar is embedded in a paragraph.

Problem 2: No Vocabulary Programme

Vocabulary appears only incidentally through verb lists. There are no thematic word sets (health, environment, technology, travel), no synonym/antonym practice, no word families, and no definition-based exercises. The exam's vocabulary section tests context inference and synonym selection — skills that require volume of reading exposure and deliberate vocabulary study.

📌Impact: Students guess on every vocabulary question. This is approximately 8–12 marks lost per exam.

Problem 3: No Spaced Repetition or Review

Your curriculum is linear: each topic is taught once and never systematically revisited. Research in cognitive science (Ebbinghaus forgetting curve) shows that without review at 1 day, 1 week, 1 month, and 3 months, students forget ~80% of new material within 30 days. Your curriculum has no built-in review units, cumulative tests, or recycling exercises.

📌Impact: This is the primary reason students forget material after completing the course.

Problem 4: Grammar Taught in Isolation

Grammar rules are taught as formulas to memorise, then drilled with single-sentence fill-in-the-blank exercises. There is no integration: tenses are not practised within conversations, conditionals are not linked to real situations, and modal verbs are not connected to speaking tasks. The exam tests grammar in context (cloze tests, paraphrasing) — a skill your students have not been trained in.

Problem 5: Missing Exam-Specific Skills

Three exam skills are entirely absent from your curriculum: Paraphrasing (requires knowing synonymous grammar structures), Idioms & Proverbs (requires a dedicated phrase bank), and Short Conversation as a tested skill (not just phrase memorisation). Also missing: Phrasal Verbs (tested in ASMO-level exams) and Gerund vs. Infinitive distinction.

Problem 6: Insufficient Assessment Design

Your curriculum has exercises but no formal diagnostic tests, no unit tests, no cumulative mid-term or final exams, and no progress tracking. Students have no way to identify their weaknesses. You have no data on which topics to reteach. Without assessment data, both teacher and student are flying blind.

Phase Three

New Curriculum Design

3.1 Design Principles

01

Spiral Learning

Every topic is introduced, then revisited at higher complexity in a later unit. Grammar feeds into reading. Reading feeds back into writing.

02

Spaced Repetition

Built-in review sessions at Day 1, Day 7, Day 21, and Day 60 after each new unit. Cumulative quizzes every 5 lessons.

03

Skill Integration

Every unit links grammar to reading, vocabulary to conversation, and structure to writing — mirroring how exams actually test English.

04

Retrieval Practice

Students recall information from memory (not re-read notes) through low-stakes quizzes, flashcard sessions, and peer-teaching activities.

05

Exam Readiness

Every unit includes at least one exam-format question type (multiple choice, cloze, paraphrase) so students are never surprised by the test format.

06

Real-World Connection

Topics are connected to authentic contexts: news snippets, dialogues, short stories, and real-life scenarios students encounter in everyday life.

C. Reading Passage — "A Lifetime of Research"

Dr. Maria Chen has devoted her life to fighting infectious diseases. Since 2005, she has worked at the World Health Organization in Geneva. She has published over 80 research papers and has spoken at conferences in more than 40 countries. Recently, her team has developed a new vaccine that could save millions of lives.

Dr. Chen has always believed that persistence is the key to success. "I have never given up, even when results were disappointing," she says. Her work has already changed the lives of thousands of patients around the world.

Comprehension Questions (exam format)

  1. How long has Dr. Chen worked at the WHO? (Specific detail)
  2. According to the passage, what has her team recently developed? (Specific detail)
  3. The word "persistence" in paragraph 2 is closest in meaning to: A. patience B. intelligence C. determination D. confidence (Vocabulary in context)
  4. Which statement about Dr. Chen is TRUE according to the passage? (True/False inference)
  5. What is the BEST title for this passage? (Main idea)

Phase Five

Lesson Plans

5.1 Lesson Plan Format

Each lesson is 60–90 minutes. Below are two complete lesson plans: one introductory grammar lesson and one exam-focused practice lesson.

Lesson Plan: B1-03A — Introducing Present Perfect 60 mins · ม.1–ม.2
Learning Objectives:
  • Students can form Present Perfect sentences (positive, negative, question) correctly.
  • Students can identify the difference between Present Perfect and Past Simple.
  • Students can use "since", "for", "already", "yet", "ever", "never" correctly.
  • Students can answer 4/5 reading comprehension questions on a Present Perfect text.
0–10 min
WARM-UP
Retrieval Starter: "Have you ever…?" Survey Students mingle and ask 3 classmates "Have you ever eaten sushi? / visited Bangkok? / watched an English movie?" Write answers on mini-whiteboard. Teacher elicits sentences naturally.
↻ This revisits B1-02 question formation from last lesson.
10–20 min
INPUT
Reading: "A Lifetime of Research" (Section C of Textbook) Students read silently (3 min), then highlight every "has/have + V.3" they find. Class shares findings. Teacher writes 5 examples on board. Students deduce: "What do these all have in common?"
20–30 min
GRAMMAR
Grammar Spotlight (Section D) — Explicit Instruction Teach rule, form, and use (10 min). Focus on: structure table, time words, error box. Students copy one personalised example sentence each. Teacher gives 3 quick-fire oral drills: "Change to Present Perfect: She went to France." → "She has been to France."
30–45 min
PRACTICE
Controlled → Semi-Free Practice (Section E) (1) Fill-in: 10 sentences using cues — 5 min solo, check in pairs.
(2) Error correction: 6 wrong sentences — find and fix — 5 min.
(3) "Timeline" activity: Given a person's life events, write 5 Present Perfect sentences. Share one with class.
45–55 min
APPLICATION
Exam-Format Practice (Section G — partial) 5-question MCQ in exam format (no notes). Students attempt independently. Peer-mark. Teacher explains wrong answers with reasoning — not just "the answer is C" but "because 'since 2005' tells us the action connects past to present."
55–60 min
CLOSE
Exit Ticket + Preview Each student writes 1 sentence using Present Perfect on a sticky note (leave at desk). Teacher scans for errors. Homework assigned: write 5 true sentences about yourself using "I have/have never…" — next class, share with partner.
Lesson Plan: B1 Exam Skills — Cloze Test Strategies 75 mins · ม.2–ม.3 / Exam-focused
Learning Objectives:
  • Students can identify whether a blank requires grammar or vocabulary.
  • Students can apply a 4-step cloze test strategy to eliminate wrong answers.
  • Students can complete a 10-blank cloze passage in under 10 minutes.
0–10 min
WARM-UP
Spaced Review: 10-Q Quiz (previous 3 units) Brief quiz on grammar from B1-01 to B1-02. Self-mark. Students record score. Teacher notes common errors for remediation. This is the +7-day spaced review for B1-02.
10–25 min
STRATEGY
Teaching Cloze Strategy: The RCES Method
  1. Read the whole passage first (get the meaning).
  2. Classify each blank: grammar blank or vocabulary blank?
  3. Eliminate 2 wrong answers immediately.
  4. Select using context + grammar rule.
Teacher demonstrates with a sample cloze on board. Think-aloud strategy modelling is essential.
25–50 min
PRACTICE
Guided → Independent Cloze Practice (1) Cloze A (guided): teacher and class solve together using RCES method — 10 blanks, 10 min.
(2) Cloze B (pair work): pairs solve with discussion — 10 blanks, 8 min.
(3) Cloze C (independent, timed): solo, exactly 8 minutes — exam simulation.
50–65 min
REVIEW
Error Analysis & Explanation Students compare Cloze C answers in groups. Each group must explain WHY each answer is correct (not just what it is). Teacher facilitates 5-min class discussion on hardest blanks. Highlight exam traps: distractors that are grammatically similar but contextually wrong.
65–75 min
CLOSE
Homework + Spaced Review Assignment Homework: 1 cloze passage (website or workbook). Students note which step (R/C/E/S) they found hardest and why — brief written reflection (3 sentences). Next class starts with sharing reflections.
