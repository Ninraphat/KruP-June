Career Advice — A Student's Dilemma

~200 words · Dialogue + Article · B1

Dialogue:

"I have an interview tomorrow and I don't know what to wear," said Nana. "You should wear something professional," replied her friend Kai. "Can I borrow your blue jacket?" asked Nana. "Of course! You can take it. But you must return it by Friday," said Kai. "Don't worry, I will. Do you think I might get the job?" Nana asked nervously. "You must be very qualified — they would be lucky to have you!"

Article — Tips for Job Interviews:

Candidates should arrive at least ten minutes early. You must not use your phone during the interview. You ought to research the company beforehand — this may impress the interviewer. If you are unsure about a question, you can ask for clarification. Candidates should also prepare questions to ask. Remember: you might feel nervous, but confidence can be learned and practised. After the interview, you should send a thank-you email within 24 hours.

Section C — Grammar Spotlight: Modal Verbs

All modal verbs, their meanings, uses, and formulas. Click each group to expand.

🔧
Key Rules for ALL Modal Verbs:
1. Modal + base verb (V1) — NEVER add -s, -ing, or -ed to the modal itself.
2. To make negative: modal + not + V1 (e.g. cannot, should not, will not)
3. To make question: Modal + Subject + V1? (e.g. Can you...? Must I...?)
4. Modal verbs have NO infinitive, gerund, or past form — use alternatives when needed.
can / could

Can & Could

Ability, permission, possibility, polite requests

›
S + can/could + V1 negative: cannot (can't) / could not (couldn't)
ABILITY
can = present ability  |  could = past ability
"She can speak three languages."  /  "He could swim when he was five."
เย็นและด้วย (ปัจจุบัน) / เคยทำได้ (อดีต)
PERMISSION
can = informal permission  |  could = more polite request
"Can I use your phone?" (informal)  /  "Could I borrow your pen?" (polite)
การขออนุญาต (could เพื่อความศึภาพดียิ่งขึ้น)
POSSIBILITY
could = theoretical possibility
"This plan could work."  /  "It could be dangerous."
ความเป็นไปได้ในทางทฐษฎี
❌
Error: "She can speaks French." ✗ → "She can speak French." ✓ — NEVER add -s after a modal.
must / have to

Must & Have to

Obligation, necessity, prohibition, deduction

›
S + must + V1 or S + have/has to + V1
OBLIGATION
must = internal/personal obligation  |  have to = external rule/requirement
"I must study tonight." (my own decision)  /  "Students have to wear uniforms." (school rule)
must = ตัดสินใจเอง / have to = กำหนดจากภายนอก
PROHIBITION
must not / mustn't = it is NOT allowed (strong prohibition)
"You must not enter this area."  /  "Students mustn't cheat on exams."
ห้ามโดยเด็ดขาด
DEDUCTION
must = logical conclusion (almost certain) from evidence
"She knows everyone here — she must be the manager."
อนุมานในความแน่นอนสูง
⚡
must not vs. don't have to:
"You must not smoke here." = FORBIDDEN (not allowed) ✗
"You don't have to come early." = NOT NECESSARY (but you can if you want) ✓
ต้องห้าม ✗   vs.   ไม่จำเป็นต้องทำ ✓
may / might

May & Might

Possibility, permission (formal), uncertainty

›
S + may/might + V1 negative: may not / might not (mightn't)
POSSIBILITY
may = 50% chance  |  might = less certain (<50%)
"It may rain today." (fairly possible)  /  "She might come, but I'm not sure." (less likely)
may = โอกาสพอสมควร / might = โอกาสน้อยกว่า
PERMISSION
may = formal permission (more polite than "can")
"May I come in?"  /  "You may leave when you finish."
การขออนุญาตแบบทางการ
💡
Degrees of certainty (present):
must be (95% sure) > should be (80%) > may be (50%) > might be (30%) > can't be (0% — impossible)
should / ought to

Should & Ought to

Advice, recommendation, expectation, criticism

›
S + should + V1 or S + ought to + V1
ADVICE
Recommendation or suggestion (weaker than "must")
"You should see a doctor."  /  "We ought to leave now."
คำแนะนำ / ข้อแนะนำ
EXPECTATION
Something that is expected to happen
"The train should arrive at 9."  /  "This should be easy."
สิ่งที่คาดว่าจะเกิดขึ้น
CRITICISM
Mild criticism about what someone does or doesn't do
"You shouldn't eat so much fast food."
คำติเตียนเล็กน้อย
would / shall / will

Would, Shall & Will

Requests, offers, preferences, conditionals, future

›
S + would + V1 | S + shall + V1 | S + will + V1
POLITE REQUEST
would = very polite request
"Would you mind helping me?"  /  "Would you like some tea?"
การขอร้องศอความช่วยเหลือแบบสุภาพ
PREFERENCE
would rather = prefer one thing over another
"I would rather stay home than go out."  /  "Would you rather have tea or coffee?"
ชอบมากกว่า / เลือกมากกว่า
CONDITIONAL
would = imaginary/hypothetical situations (Type 2 conditional)
"If I were rich, I would travel the world."
เนื้อหาสมมติ (เทคนิคแบบที่ 2)
OFFER/SUGGEST
shall = offer or suggestion (with I/we)
"Shall I open the window?"  /  "Shall we go now?"
เสนอ / สานไพถามความ
USED TO
would = past habits or repeated past actions
"When I was young, I would play in the park every day."
นิสัยในอดีตที่ทำซ้ำมาก (= used to)
need not / dare not

Needn't & Dare not

Lack of necessity, lack of courage

›
NO NECESSITY
need not / needn't = it is NOT necessary (do not need to do it)
"You needn't bring food — it will be provided."  /  "She need not come early."
ไม่จำเป็นต้องทำ
DARE NOT
dare not / daren't = not brave enough to do something
"He dare not tell his parents the truth."
ไม่กล้าทำ

Section D — Perfect Modal Verbs

Modal + have + V3 — talking about the past with modals. A very common exam topic!

🔧
Formula:   Modal + have + V3 (past participle)
Perfect modals are used to talk about situations in the PAST — expressing deduction, criticism, regret, or possibility about a past event.
Perfect ModalMeaningThaiExample
must have + V3Strong deduction about the past (almost certain)ต้องเป็นแน่นอนว่าเกิดขึ้นแล้ว"She is not here — she must have left already."
can't have + V3Strong negative deduction (impossible)ไม่มีทางเป็นไปได้"He can't have stolen it — he was with me all day."
should have + V3Past advice/criticism — the right thing to do (but didn't)ควรจะทำแต่ไม่ได้ทำ (เสียดาย)"You should have studied harder." (but you didn't)
shouldn't have + V3Past criticism — the wrong thing was doneไม่ควรทำ (แต่ทำไปแล้ว)"You shouldn't have eaten all the cake!" (but you did)
might have + V3Weak past possibility (unsure)อาจเป็นไปได้ในอดีต"She might have missed the bus." (not sure)
may have + V3Medium past possibilityโอกาสพอสมควรในอดีต"He may have forgotten about the meeting."
could have + V3Past ability/possibility that didn't happenทำได้แต่ไม่ได้ทำ"I could have won if I had tried harder."
would have + V3Hypothetical past (Type 3 conditional)คงจะเกิดขึ้น ถ้า...(แต่ไม่เกิด)"If I had studied, I would have passed."
needn't have + V3Did something that was NOT necessaryทำไปแล้วโดยไม่จำเป็น"You needn't have cooked so much food." (you did but it wasn't needed)
❌
Common Error — "should have went" vs "should have gone":
After modal + have, ALWAYS use V3 (past participle) — never V2.
"You should have went." ✗  →  "You should have gone." ✓
"She must have took it." ✗  →  "She must have taken it." ✓
⚡
didn't have to vs. needn't have:
"I didn't have to go." = It was not necessary, so I didn't go. (I made the right choice)
"I needn't have gone." = I went, but it turned out to be unnecessary. (I wasted my time)
✏

B must

✓ "Must" = strong obligation (law/rule). "Should" is weaker (advice only).
✗ Strong legal obligation = "must". "Should" is for advice, not law.
2 _____ I use your phone? [polite request for permission]
A Should
B Must
C Could
D Would
✓ "Could I...?" = polite request for permission. "Can I?" is more informal. "Would" needs "you" (Would you...?).
✗ Polite permission request = "Could I...?" or "May I...?"
3 You _____ tell anyone this secret! [strong prohibition]
A don't have to
B must not
C might not
D should not
✓ "Must not" = strong prohibition (not allowed). "Don't have to" means not necessary (very different!).
✗ PROHIBITION = "must not". "Don't have to" = not necessary (NOT prohibited).
4 Look at those dark clouds — it _____ rain soon. [medium possibility]
A must
B may
C should
D would
✓ "May" = medium possibility (~50%). "Might" is also acceptable (less certain). "Must" = near-certainty deduction.
✗ Medium possibility = "may" (50%) or "might" (less than 50%). "Must" = almost certain deduction.
5 The keys are on the table — they _____ be in your bag. [impossibility / negative deduction]
A can't
B might not
C shouldn't
D wouldn't
✓ "Can't be" = logical impossibility/negative deduction (almost certain something is NOT true).
✗ "Can't be" = strong negative deduction (impossible). "Might not" shows less certainty.
6 You _____ bring anything — food and drinks will be provided. [no necessity]
A must not
B don't have to
C cannot
D might not
✓ "Don't have to" = not necessary (but you can if you want). "Must not" = forbidden. These are opposite meanings!
✗ No necessity = "don't have to" / "needn't". "Must not" = PROHIBITED (forbidden).
7 I _____ rather drink water than soda. [preference]
A would
B should
C may
D must
✓ "Would rather" = preference between two choices. Pattern: would rather + V1 + than + V1.
✗ Preference = "would rather". "I would rather... than..."
8 _____ I help you carry those bags? [offer]
A Shall
B Would
C Must
D May
✓ "Shall I...?" = offering to do something (with I/we). "Shall we...?" = suggesting to do something together.
✗ Offering help with "I" = "Shall I...?" Suggesting together = "Shall we...?"

B should have

A shouldn't have

4

Error Correction

Each sentence has ONE modal verb error. Find and fix it.

📌 Click "Show Correction" to see the fix and the rule.
1. She can speaks three languages fluently. ✗ speaks
✓ She can speak three languages fluently.
Modal verbs are ALWAYS followed by the base verb (V1). Never add -s, -ing, or -ed.
2. You don't have to smoke in this building — it's forbidden. ✗ don't have to
✓ You must not smoke in this building.
"Forbidden" = prohibition = "must not". "Don't have to" = not necessary (totally different meaning).
3. He should have went to the doctor sooner. ✗ went
✓ He should have gone to the doctor sooner.
After "modal + have", always use V3 (past participle). "Gone" is V3 of "go", not "went" (V2).
4. I would rather to stay home tonight. ✗ to stay
✓ I would rather stay home tonight.
"Would rather" is followed directly by the base verb (V1), NOT "to + V1". No "to" needed!
5. You must to finish your homework before watching TV. ✗ to finish
✓ You must finish your homework before watching TV.
Modal verbs are NEVER followed by "to + V1". Just: modal + base verb. (Exception: "ought to" and "have to" do use "to")
6. It is being very cold today — you should wear a jacket. ✗ is being
✓ It must be very cold today — you should wear a jacket.
"Must be" expresses logical deduction (strong certainty). "Is being" suggests temporary behaviour — wrong for weather/temperature.

Section F — Unit Review

Summary of all modal meanings + self-evaluation checklist.

🚨
Top 8 Exam Traps:
  1. can speaks ✗ → can speak ✓ — modal + base verb (V1), never -s/-ing/-ed
  2. must not (not necessary) ✗ → don't have to ✓ — "must not" = forbidden
  3. should have went ✗ → should have gone ✓ — modal + have + V3, never V2
  4. would rather to stay ✗ → would rather stay ✓ — no "to" after "would rather"
  5. must to finish ✗ → must finish ✓ — no "to" after most modals
  6. Confusing needn't have and shouldn't have — needn't = unnecessary; shouldn't = wrong
  7. Confusing could (ability/possibility) and was able to (specific past achievement)
  8. I'd rather you don't ✗ → I'd rather you didn't ✓ — use past tense after "I'd rather you"

must have + V3Strong past deduction"She must have left already."
should have + V3Past advice — right thing not done"You should have studied harder."
shouldn't have + V3Past criticism — wrong thing was done"You shouldn't have eaten all the cake."
could have + V3Past ability/opportunity not used"I could have won if I'd tried harder."
needn't have + V3Action done, but was unnecessary"You needn't have cooked so much."
