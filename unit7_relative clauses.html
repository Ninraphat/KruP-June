The Architect of Change

~180 words · Biography · B1

Dr Amara is a scientist who has dedicated her life to environmental research. She grew up in a small village where clean water was scarce. This early experience, which shaped her entire career, inspired her to study environmental engineering.

At university, she met Professor Chan, whose groundbreaking research on water purification had already changed thousands of lives. She became his student, and the laboratory where they worked together became famous worldwide.

Dr Amara developed a low-cost filter that can purify water using sunlight. The device, which costs less than ten dollars to produce, has now been distributed to over fifty countries. The communities that use her device report a dramatic improvement in health.

Last year was the year when she received the Global Innovation Prize — an award that recognises scientists who make a real difference in the world.

✓ "Whose" is used to show possession. "Whose research" = the research belonging to Professor Chan. "Whose" replaces "his/her/their" before a noun.

Section C — Relative Pronouns

🔧
What is a relative clause?
A relative clause is a type of subordinate clause that modifies (describes) a noun. It usually comes directly after the noun it modifies (the antecedent).
"The man who called you is my uncle." — "who called you" describes "the man"
who

WHO

For people — as subject or object of the relative clause

›
👥
Use who when referring to people. It can be the subject or object of the relative clause. (Thai: ษึ่ง / ด้วย = คนที่)
Subject: The student who won the prize is in my class.
นักเรียนที่ได้รังวัลอยู่ในใชมันเดียวกันวันฑ์ (who = ประธานของอนุประโยค)
Object: The teacher who I admire most taught us last year.
ครูที่นโย෽ร่วมชมเชยตร่วบสอนเราเมื่อปีที่แล้ว (who = กรรมของอนุประโยค)
💡
When "who" is the OBJECT, it can be replaced by whom (formal) or omitted entirely in defining clauses:
"The teacher whom I admire…" (formal) / "The teacher I admire…" (omitted)
whom

WHOM

For people — formal object form of "who"

›
👥
Whom is the formal/written object form of "who". Use it after prepositions or when the relative pronoun is the object. (Thai: ษึ่ง...เขา / ตัวไหน = เงื่อนไขมาตรฐาน)
The man whom she married is a doctor.
พ่อยดีของเธอเป็นหมอ (whom = กรรมఊียมภาพสูง)
The professor to whom I wrote replied immediately. (preposition + whom)
ศาสตราจารย์ที่นโย෽เขียนถึงตอบด้วย (whom หลัง preposition)
❌
Exam trap: In modern/informal English, "whom" is often replaced by "who". But after a preposition, "whom" is required: "the person to whom I spoke" (NOT "to who").
which

WHICH

For things, animals, or entire clauses

›
📚
Use which for things, animals, or to refer to an entire preceding clause. (Thai: ษึ่ง = สิ่งของ / สัตว์)
For things: The book which I borrowed was fascinating.
หนังสือที่นโย෽ยืมมาน่าโชคมาก
For entire clause: She passed the exam, which surprised everyone.
เธอสอบผ่าน ษึ่งทำให้ทุกคนแปลกใจ ("which" refers to the entire idea)
⚡
When "which" refers to an ENTIRE clause (not just a noun), it is ALWAYS non-defining and needs a comma: "She arrived late, which was very unusual."
whose

WHOSE

For people and things — shows possession

›
👥
Use whose to show possession — for both people AND things. It replaces "his/her/their/its" before a noun. (Thai: ที่มี...พ่เขา / ที่มี...บอกของฒ์)
For people: The girl whose bag was stolen reported it to the police.
เด็ก E2;ุที่หาย฿ฤิดไปแจ้งตำรวจ (whose = เจ้าของ௛)
For things: The company whose products we use is based in Japan.
บริษัทที่เราใช้ E2;ลิตภัณķD;ธ์ตั้งอยู่ใน൪ี่ปุ่น (whose สำหรับสิ่งของ)
❌
Common error: "The student which bag was stolen…" ✗ → Use "whose" for possession, NOT "which": "The student whose bag was stolen…" ✓
where

WHERE

For places

›
📍
Use where when the antecedent is a place. It replaces "in which / at which / on which". (Thai: ที่ศึ่ง = สถานที่)
The city where I was born is very beautiful.
เมืองที่นโย෽เกิดมาๅนดีมาก (where = in which)
The restaurant where we had dinner has closed down.
ร้านอาหารที่เราไปทานข้าวเย็นB9;ร้เลย
💡
"Where" = "in which" / "at which". Both are correct:
"The town where she lives" = "The town in which she lives"
when

WHEN

For time expressions

›
🕐
Use when when the antecedent is a time expression (day, year, moment, time, etc.). It replaces "in which / on which / at which". (Thai: เมื่อ = เวลา)
I remember the day when we first met.
นโย෽จำวันที่เราพบกันครั้งแรกได้
2020 was the year when everything changed.
ปี 2020 เป็นปีที่ทุกอย่างเปลี่ยนแปลงไป
that

THAT

For people OR things — ONLY in defining clauses

›
💡
Use that for people OR things, but ONLY in defining (restrictive) relative clauses. It can NEVER be used in non-defining clauses. (Thai: ที่ / ธ็ม / ษึ่ง = เท่านั้นใน E1;ระโยคจำเป็น)
For people: The boy that broke the window ran away.
(= The boy who broke the window ran away.)
For things: The car that I want to buy is very expensive.
(= The car which I want to buy is very expensive.)
❌
NEVER use "that" in non-defining clauses:
"My sister, that lives in Bangkok, is a nurse." ✗
→ "My sister, who lives in Bangkok, is a nurse." ✓
🔥
"That" is PREFERRED (over "which") after:
• Superlatives: "the best film that I've ever seen"
• "the only": "the only person that can help"
• "the first/last": "the last train that leaves tonight"
• "all, everything, nothing, something": "everything that you need"

Section D — Defining vs. Non-Defining

The most important distinction in relative clauses — and the most tested in exams.

🟢 DEFINING (Restrictive)

Identifies WHICH person/thing we mean. Without it, the sentence loses its meaning.

No commas. Can use who, which, that, whose, where, when.

"The woman who called you is outside."
(WHICH woman? — the one who called you. Essential info.)
"Students who work hard usually succeed."
(WHICH students? — the ones who work hard.)

🟠 NON-DEFINING (Non-Restrictive)

Adds extra information about an already-identified noun. Remove it and the sentence still makes sense.

Always has commas. Use who, which, whose, where, when — NEVER "that".

"My mother, who is a doctor, works at this hospital."
(We already know which mother — "who is a doctor" is extra info.)
"Paris, which is the capital of France, is beautiful."
(Paris is already identified. The clause adds extra info.)
Feature🟢 Defining🟠 Non-Defining
CommasNO commasALWAYS commas (before and after)
Can use "that"?✓ Yes✗ NEVER
Can omit pronoun?✓ Yes (if object)✗ Never
Information typeEssential — identifies WHICH oneExtra — adds information
Remove the clause?Sentence loses meaningSentence still makes sense
Pronouns allowedwho, whom, which, that, whose, where, whenwho, whom, which, whose, where, when (NOT that)
❌
The 3 Most Common Errors:
1. Using "that" in a non-defining clause: "My sister, that is a nurse…" ✗ → use "who"
2. Forgetting commas in non-defining clauses: "My sister who is a nurse works here." ✗ (if this is non-defining, add commas)
3. Confusing "whose" with "who's": "The man who's car was stolen…" ✗ → "who's" = "who is" — use whose for possession

Omitting the Relative Pronoun

💡
A relative pronoun can be omitted ONLY when it is the object in a defining clause.
"The film that I watched was amazing." → "The film I watched was amazing." ✓ (omitted)
But if it is the SUBJECT, it CANNOT be omitted:
"The film that won the award was amazing." → ✗ Cannot omit — "that" is the subject here.
SentenceRole of PronounCan Omit?
"The book that I read was interesting."Object (I = subject, book = object)✓ Yes → "The book I read…"
"The book that won the prize was interesting."Subject (book = subject)✗ No
"The man who she loves is kind."Object (she = subject)✓ Yes → "The man she loves…"
"The man who helped me is kind."Subject (man = subject)✗ No

Five activities covering all relative pronouns and both clause types.

1

Choose the Correct Relative Pronoun

Select who, whom, which, whose, where, when, or that.

📌 Think about the antecedent (the noun before the blank) and the pronoun's role in the clause.
1 The woman _____ called yesterday is my aunt.
A who
B which
C whose
D where
✓ "Who" — the antecedent is "the woman" (a person). "Who" is the subject of the relative clause (she called).
✗ The antecedent "the woman" is a person → use "who" (or "that" in defining clauses).
2 This is the village _____ my grandfather was born.
A which
B that
C where
D when
✓ "Where" — "the village" is a place. "Where" = "in which". "The village where my grandfather was born."
✗ The antecedent "village" is a place → use "where".
3 The scientist _____ research changed the world received a Nobel Prize.
A who
B which
C whose
D whom
✓ "Whose" — the research BELONGS to the scientist (possession). "Whose research" = "his/her research".
✗ "Research" belongs to "the scientist" → use "whose" to show possession.
4 I remember the summer _____ we went to Chiang Mai together.
A which
B where
C when
D whose
✓ "When" — "the summer" is a time expression. "When" = "in which" for time.
✗ The antecedent "the summer" is a time expression → use "when".
5 The book _____ she recommended is out of stock.
A who
B that
C whose
D where
✓ "That" (or "which") — "the book" is a thing. "That" is the object of "recommended" in a defining clause.
✗ "The book" is a thing → use "which" or "that". "Who" is only for people.
6 She introduced me to her brother, _____ is a famous chef.
A who
B that
C which
D whose
✓ "Who" — non-defining clause (comma before), so "that" is NOT allowed. "Her brother" is a person → "who".
✗ The comma shows this is non-defining → "that" is NEVER used in non-defining clauses. Use "who" for a person.
2

Fill in the Correct Relative Pronoun

Use who, whom, which, whose, where, when, or that.

📌 Click Reveal to check your answer and see the rule.
1. The hotel where / in which we stayed had a beautiful pool.
✓ where (or "in which") — "the hotel" is a place → use "where".
2. The man whose wallet I found was very grateful.
✓ whose — the wallet BELONGS to the man. "Whose wallet" = "his wallet".
3. My grandmother, who is 85 years old, still walks five kilometres every day.
✓ who — non-defining clause (comma), person → "who". NEVER "that" in non-defining clauses.
4. The film that / which won the Academy Award was directed by a Thai filmmaker.
✓ that or which — defining clause for a thing. Both are acceptable. "That" is preferred after superlatives/defining contexts.
5. I'll never forget the moment when she told me the good news.
✓ when — "the moment" is a time expression → use "when".
6. The professor to whom I sent my essay has not replied yet.
✓ whom — after a preposition ("to"), always use "whom" not "who".
7. Chiang Mai is a city where / which many tourists visit every year.
✓ where (or "which") — "city" is a place. "Which" is also possible if tourists "visit it", making "which" the object.
8. The company, which was founded in 1990, now employs over 5,000 people.
✓ which — non-defining clause (comma), thing → "which". NEVER "that" in non-defining clauses.
3

Combine the Sentences

Join the two sentences using a relative clause.

📌 Click Reveal to see the combined sentence and rule.
1. The girl is my neighbour. Her cat won a prize.
→ The girl whose cat won a prize is my neighbour.
✓ "Whose cat won a prize" — "her cat" → "whose cat" (possession).
2. The restaurant is very popular. We had dinner there last night.
→ The restaurant where we had dinner last night is very popular.
✓ "where we had dinner last night" — "there" refers to a place → use "where".
3. Shakespeare was a playwright. He wrote Hamlet.
→ Shakespeare, who wrote Hamlet, was a famous playwright.
✓ "who wrote Hamlet" — non-defining (Shakespeare already identified, commas needed). Person → "who". NOT "that" in non-defining clauses.
4. I found a wallet. It contained a lot of money.
→ I found a wallet that / which contained a lot of money.
✓ "that / which contained a lot of money" — defining clause (which wallet?), thing → "that" or "which".
5. I will never forget 2020. Everything changed in 2020.
→ I will never forget 2020, when everything changed.
✓ "when everything changed" — non-defining (2020 already identified, comma), time expression → "when".
4

Error Correction

Each sentence has ONE relative clause error. Find and fix it.

📌 Click "Show Correction" to see the fix and the rule.
1. The man which called you is my uncle. ✗ which
✓ The man who called you is my uncle.
"Which" is for things. "The man" is a person → use "who" (or "that").
2. My sister, that lives in Phuket, is a doctor. ✗ that
✓ My sister, who lives in Phuket, is a doctor.
Commas = non-defining clause. "That" is NEVER used in non-defining clauses. Use "who" for people.
3. The student who's book was stolen reported it to the teacher. ✗ who's
✓ The student whose book was stolen reported it to the teacher.
"Who's" = "who is". For possession, use "whose" (no apostrophe).
4. Bangkok is the city which I was born. ✗ which
✓ Bangkok is the city where I was born. (or "in which")
"The city" is a place → use "where" (= "in which"). "Which" alone cannot replace "in which" without the preposition.
5. The phone which screen is cracked still works. ✗ which screen
✓ The phone whose screen is cracked still works.
The screen BELONGS to the phone → use "whose" even for things (not "which").
6. The Eiffel Tower, that was built in 1889, is in Paris. ✗ that
✓ The Eiffel Tower, which was built in 1889, is in Paris.
Commas = non-defining clause. "That" is NEVER used in non-defining clauses. Use "which" for things.

Section F — Unit Review

Summary of all relative pronouns + self-evaluation checklist.

🚨
Top 8 Exam Traps:
  1. "The man which called…" ✗ → "The man who called…" ✓ — "which" is for things, not people
  2. "My sister, that lives in…" ✗ → "My sister, who lives in…" ✓ — "that" NEVER in non-defining clauses
  3. "Who's bag" ✗ → "Whose bag" ✓ — "who's" = "who is"; "whose" = possession
  4. "The city which I was born" ✗ → "The city where I was born" ✓ — place → "where"
  5. Forgetting commas in non-defining clauses: "My father who is 60 is still working." → add commas if non-defining
  6. "To who I spoke" ✗ → "To whom I spoke" ✓ — preposition + "whom"
  7. "The phone which screen is cracked" ✗ → "The phone whose screen is cracked" ✓ — possession for things = "whose"
  8. After superlatives, use "that" not "which": "the best film which" ✗ → "the best film that" ✓
